If you're seeing tablets, capsules, or shots labeled **"Cat's Claw"** or **"Rhynchophylline"** at smoke shops or gas stations, read this before you take one.

## The botanical relationship (this is more nuanced than "totally unrelated")
Cat's claw (*Uncaria tomentosa*) and kratom (*Mitragyna speciosa*) [are both in the Rubiaceae family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitraphylline), which also includes coffee. They're botanical cousins, not the same plant. They share a few alkaloids in common, particularly **mitraphylline** and **rhynchophylline**, both of which [appear in cat's claw and in trace amounts in kratom](https://www.kratomscience.com/mitragyna-speciosa-kratom-alkaloids-effects/).

This is why marketers can technically claim some chemical relationship. But the relationship is being used as cover for something else.

## Real cat's claw is not psychoactive in the way kratom is
*Uncaria tomentosa* is [traditionally used as an anti-inflammatory and immune-support herb](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12296035/). The alkaloids marketers point to when they invoke the cat's-claw name, **mitraphylline and rhynchophylline**, [showed no measurable binding at opioid receptors](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pharmacology/articles/10.3389/fphar.2026.1763551/full) in a 2026 receptor study. On their own they don't produce euphoria, sedation, or opioid pain relief. If a "Cat's Claw" product is hitting you like an opioid, something else is in it.

That same study found one exception, and it's the one now showing up in these products: **corynoxine B**.

## What's really in these products
Testing on the newer "Cat's Claw" product lines has reported:
- **Corynoxine B** — a mu-opioid-active oxindole alkaloid (see below)
- **[MGM-15](/compounds/mgm15)** (semi-synthetic opioid derived from [7-OH](/compounds/7-oh))
- **undisclosed synthetic compounds** with unknown pharmacology

Companies are launching these product lines under the "Cat's Claw" name to **evade regulatory scrutiny** as [7-OH](/compounds/7-oh) and related compounds get scheduled. The botanical-cousin relationship is being used as marketing cover. It's the same playbook tianeptine used with names like "Tianaa" and "Zaza", benign label, potent synthetic inside.

## Corynoxine B: the alkaloid that makes the cover story work
Corynoxine B is a minor **oxindole alkaloid** that occurs naturally in kratom itself and in the *Uncaria* (cat's-claw) genus. Unlike the inert marketing-cover alkaloids above, it's opioid-active: in the [2026 receptor study](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pharmacology/articles/10.3389/fphar.2026.1763551/full) it bound the **mu-opioid receptor with moderate affinity (Ki ≈ 118 nM, in the same range as [7-OH](/compounds/7-oh))** and drove **G-protein signaling as a high-efficacy partial agonist** (functional EC₅₀ ≈ 193 nM, E<sub>max</sub> ≈ 81%), with negligible activity at the kappa and delta receptors. It acts like an opioid at the receptor most responsible for opioid effects and opioid dependence.

That combination is exactly what makes it useful as a gap ingredient:

- **It's a real cat's-claw / kratom alkaloid**, so the botanical-cover label isn't a total fabrication.
- **It's opioid-active**, so the product does something.
- **It is not named in any of the July 2026 DEA scheduling notices.** The notices cover 7-OH above a threshold, mitragynine pseudoindoxyl, [MGM-15](/compounds/mgm15), and [MGM-16](/compounds/mgm16) (see [The Federal 7-OH Ban](/compounds/7-oh-ban)). Corynoxine B is a different molecule, and because the 7-OH rule is written around 7-hydroxymitragynine content specifically, a corynoxine-B product can stay under that threshold while still delivering opioid effects.

None of that makes it safe. There's no human safety, dosing, or long-term data on isolated corynoxine B, and a product built around it carries the same opioid-class dependence and withdrawal risk as any other mu agonist.

## Cat's Claw as a synthetic-chemistry starting point
[Researchers have already demonstrated precursor-directed biosynthesis with *Uncaria guianensis* (a cat's claw species) to produce novel oxindole alkaloid analogues, including fluorinated versions](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6683290/). That's the same kind of structural modification, adding fluorine to a kratom-family alkaloid, that medicinal chemistry programs have used to produce more potent derivatives of [MGM-15](/compounds/mgm15) and related compounds in the published literature. The cat's claw scaffold is being looked at as a starting point for novel synthetic compounds, and there's nothing stopping unregulated chemists from doing the same work and selling the results under the "Cat's Claw" name.

In other words: real cat's claw extract is benign, but products marketed as "Cat's Claw" run from opioid-active natural alkaloids like corynoxine B to synthetic modifications that have nothing to do with the traditional herb.

## Clinical implications
- **Opioid-class dependence, tolerance, and withdrawal.** Whether the active is corynoxine B, [MGM-15](/compounds/mgm15), or something undisclosed, these are mu-opioid agonists and behave like opioids in the body.
- **Won't show on standard drug tests.** Probation, employment, treatment program panels, none of them are looking for these compounds.
- **No human safety data.** Dose, half-life, long-term effects, drug interactions, all unknown.
- **What's in the bottle isn't always what's on the label**, and batch-to-batch variation is significant even within the same brand.
