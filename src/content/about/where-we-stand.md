Some people consume kratom or 7-OH products without ever crossing into dependence — sticking to one or two of the labeled per-serving doses, not escalating, taking breaks when they need them. That's a legitimate consumer experience, and nothing on the rest of this site is trying to argue otherwise. If you landed here from a Reddit link, a search, or a friend pointing you our way and that's the picture you recognize: welcome. Look around if you want, but most of what's here is written for someone else.

This site is written for the other reality: people whose use of these compounds went sideways, often without their realizing what category they were in. Dependence on concentrated 7-OH builds quickly. The withdrawal hits like classical opioid withdrawal. The post-acute symptoms drag on for weeks or months, sometimes longer. Family members watching from outside need their own language for what they're seeing. The framing across this site (the doses, the warnings, the way 7-OH is described in opioid terms) is calibrated for that audience.

Some of it will read as one-sided, alarmist, or unfamiliar to readers whose experience is different. That isn't a flaw. It's a function of who the writing is for. Two things can be true at the same time: a substance can be manageable for someone who stays at one or two servings and takes breaks, and devastating for someone who escalated without noticing. The pages here lean hard into the second picture because that's where the practical decisions get made — at 3 a.m., in acute withdrawal, scared, looking for something concrete to do. Hedged language is useless in that moment. The same words read by someone who's never been there can sound like overstatement of a danger they don't experience. Both readings are honest.

## On bans and regulation

The site does not advocate for or against bans, schedules, or restrictions on kratom, 7-OH, or any related compound. Nobody pays us, we don't pay anyone, and we have no relationship with any regulatory advocacy of any flavor. As an empirical matter, prohibition-style approaches to substance availability have a complicated track record. The mainstream policy literature documents both intended effects (enforcement can raise prices and reduce availability under some conditions) and well-studied unintended consequences: markets going underground, contamination of supply, and the "iron law of prohibition," where the available product gets more concentrated rather than less ([Reuter and Trautmann, RAND 2009](https://www.rand.org/pubs/technical_reports/TR706.html)). That last pattern is exactly the dynamic that produced 7-OH itself — regulatory and market pressure on kratom drew the minor alkaloid out of the leaf and concentrated it into a distinct, more dangerous product class ([Alsbrook et al., 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12671409/)). Those are empirical observations, not a political stance. Reasonable people hold different positions on what should actually happen here.

The site does have a preference, stated here for context rather than as advocacy: a regulated supplement market (with mandatory testing, validation, manufacturing standards, contamination protocols, and accurate labeling) would serve readers better than either the current largely-unregulated retail landscape or an outright ban. That preference is what most of the people we've talked with have arrived at after coming through this. **It is not an advocacy position.** The site doesn't lobby, doesn't endorse specific legislation or agencies, and doesn't align with any regulatory campaign. If the policy landscape moves in any direction (toward more regulation, toward bans, toward something else), the site will continue to do exactly what it does now, which is help people in front of recovery decisions navigate them.

Readers from any of these communities (responsible users, kratom advocates, anti-ban activists, people in recovery, family members, clinicians) are welcome to use the site, share it, link it, or push back on it. The site's focus is recovery, not advocacy, not industry, not policy. That focus is the reason these pages exist and the reason they stay useful.
